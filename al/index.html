Differentiation

  1. Understand and use the derivative of \(f(x)\) as the gradient of the tangent to the graph of \(y = f(x)\) at a general point \((x, y)\); the gradient of the tangent as a limit; interpretation as a rate of change;
  2. sketching the gradient function for a given curve;
  3. differentiation from first principles for small positive integer powers of \(x\) and for \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\)
  4. Understand and use the second derivative as the rate of change of gradient; connection to convex and concave sections of curves and points of inflection.
  5. Differentiate \(x^n\), for rational values of \(n\), and related constant multiples, sums and differences.

Exponentials, logarithms

  1. Know and use the function \(a^x\) and its graph, where \(a\) is positive.
  2. Know and use the function \(e^x\) and its graph.
  3. Know that the gradient of \(e^{kx}\) is equal to \(ke^{kx}\) and hence understand why the exponential model is suitable in many applications.
  4. Know and use the definition of \(\log_a x\) as the inverse of \(a^x\), where \(a\) is positive and \(x \geq 0\).
  5. Know and use the function \(\ln x\) and its graph.
  6. Know and use \(\ln x\) as the inverse function of \(e^x\).
  7. Understand and use the laws of logarithms:
  8. \(\log_a x + \log_a y = \log_a (xy)\)
  9. \(\log_a x - \log_a y = \log_a (\frac{x}{y})\)
  10. \(\log_a x^k = k\log_a x\)
  11. Solve equations of the form \(a^x = b\)
  12. Understand and use exponential growth and decay; use in modelling (examples may include the use of \(e\) in continuous compound interest, radioactive decay, drug concentration decay, exponential growth as a model for population growth); consideration of limitations and refinements of exponential models.
Textbook: Year 1 Chapter 14.1 - 14.7

Statistics

  1. Understand and use the terms "population" and "sample", including the advantages and disadvantages of a census versus a sample.
  2. Use samples to make informal inferences about the population.
  3. Understand and use sampling techniques, including simple random sampling, stratified sampling, systematic sampling, quota sampling, and opportunity sampling.
  4. Select or critique sampling techniques in the context of solving a statistical problem, including understanding that different samples can lead to different conclusions about the population.
  5. Interpret diagrams (histograms, frequency polygons, box and whisker plots including outliers, and cumulative frequency diagrams) for single variable data, including understanding that area in a histogram represents frequency.
  6. Connect to probability distributions.
  7. Recognise and interpret possible outliers in data sets and statistical diagrams. Rules for outliers will be specified in the question.
  8. Select or critique data presentation techniques in the context of a statistical problem, including drawing simple inferences and giving interpretations to measures of central tendency and variation.
  9. Be able to clean data, including dealing with missing data, errors and outliers e.g. identifying outliers on a box plot or scatter diagram.
Textbook: Applied Year 1 Chapter 1 (recap chapter 3)

Statistics

  1. Interpret scatter diagrams and regression lines for bivariate data, including recognition of scatter diagrams which include distinct sections of the population (calculations involving regression lines are excluded). Familiarity with the terms explanatory (independent) and response (dependent) variables.
  2. Use of interpolation and the dangers of extrapolation. Variables other than \(x\) and \(y\) may be used.
  3. Understand informal interpretation of correlation. Use of terms such as positive, negative, zero, strong and weak are expected.
  4. Understand that correlation does not imply causation.
  5. Understand and use mutually exclusive and independent events when calculating probabilities. Venn diagrams or tree diagrams may be used. Set notation to describe events may be used.
  6. Link to discrete and continuous distributions. Understand that area under the curve represents probability in the case of a continuous distribution.
  7. Understand and use conditional probability, including the use of tree diagrams, Venn diagrams, two-way tables.
  8. Modelling with probability, including critiquing assumptions made and the likely effect of more realistic assumptions. For example, questioning the assumption that a die or coin is fair.

Circles

  1. Understand and use the coordinate geometry of the circle including using the equation of a circle in the form \((x – a)^2 + (y – b)^2 = r^2\) and \(x^2 + y^2 + fx + gy + c = 0\)

  3. Use of the following properties: the angle in a semicircle is a right angle; the perpendicular from the centre to a chord bisects the chord; the radius of a circle at a given point on its circumference is perpendicular to the tangent to the circle at that point.

Sequences and series

  1. Work with sequences including those given by a formula for the \(n\)th term and those generated by a simple relation of the form \(x_{n+1} = f(x_n)\); increasing sequences e.g. \(u_n = 2^n\); decreasing sequences e.g. \(u_n = \dfrac{1}{3n+1}\); periodic sequences e.g. \(u_{n+1} = \dfrac{1}{u_n}\).
  2. Understand and use sigma notation for sums of series.
  3. Understand and work with arithmetic sequences and series, including the formulae for \(n\)th term and the sum to \(n\) terms (proof required)
  4. Understand and work with geometric sequences and series, including the formulae for the \(n\)th term and the sum of a finite geometric series (proof required); the sum to infinity of a convergent geometric series, including the use of \(|r| < 1\); modulus notation
  5. Use sequences and series in modelling.
