Complex numbers

  1. Know the meaning of "real part", "imaginary part", "conjugate", "modulus" and "argument" of a complex number
  2. Be able to express a complex number \(z\) in either cartesian form or modulus-argument form, and convert between the two forms
  3. Understand and be able to use the notation: \(z, z^*, \mathrm{Re}(z), \mathrm{Im}(z), \mathrm{arg}(z), |z|\)
  4. Uderstand and be able to use the exponential form of a complex number
  5. Be able to carry out basic arithmetic operations on complex numbers in both cartesian and modulus-argument forms
  6. Be able to find algebraically the two square roots of a complex number
  7. Be able to use and interpret Argand diagrams
  8. Understand the geometrical effects of taking the conjugate of a complex number, and adding and subtracting two complex numbers
  9. Understand the geometrical effects of multiplying and dividing two complex numbers
  10. Know and be able to use Euler’s formula \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\)
  11. Be able to illustrate equations and inequalities involving complex numbers by means of loci in an Argand diagram
  12. Understand and be able to use set notation in the context of loci

Matrices

  1. Be able to find the determinant of a \(2\times 2\) or \(3\times3\) matrix with and without a calculator (including notation)
  2. Know that the determinant of a \(2\times 2\) matrix is the area scale factor of the transformation defined by that matrix, and that the determinant of a \(3\times3\) matrix is the volume scale factor of the transformation defined by that matrix, including the effect on the orientation of the image. (for 3D determinants, learners do not need to understand the geometric interpretation of a change in orientation)
  3. Understand and be able to use singular (determinant is 0) and non singular matrices, including the significance of a zero determinant
  4. Know and be able to use the result that \(\mathrm{det}(\mathrm{\textbf{AB}}) = \mathrm{det}(\mathrm{\textbf{A}}) \times\mathrm{det}(\mathrm{\textbf{B}})\)
  5. Be able to find and use the inverse of a non-singular \(2\times2\) or \(3\times3\) matrix with and without a calculator
  6. Understand and use simple properties of inverse matrices e.g. \((\mathrm{\textbf{AB}})^{-1} = \mathrm{\textbf{B}}^{-1}\mathrm{\textbf{A}}^{-1}\)
  7. Understand and be able to use the connection between inverse matrices and inverse transformations
  8. Be able to solve two or three linear simultaneous equations in two or three variables by the use of an inverse matrix, where a unique solution exists

Regression

  1. Interpret scatter diagrams and regression lines for bivariate data, including recognition of scatter diagrams which include distinct sections of the population. Students should be familiar with the terms explanatory and response variables.
  2. Use of interpolation and the dangers of extrapolation. Variables other than \(x\) and \(y\) may be used. Change of variable may be required e.g. using logarithms
  3. Understand informal interpretation of correlation ("positive", "negative", "zero", "strong", "weak") and that correlation does not equal causation
  4. Understand and use mutually exclusive and independent events when calculating probabilities. Venn/tree diagrams may be used. Set notation to describe events may be used.
  5. Link to discrete and continuous distributions. Students should understand that area under the curve represents probability in the case of a continuous distribution.
  6. Understand and use conditional probability, including the use of tree/venn diagrams and two-way tables
  7. Modelling with probability, including critiquing assumptions (e.g. a coin is fair) and the likely effect of more realistic assumptions
